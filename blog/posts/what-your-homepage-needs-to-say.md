A new visitor lands on your homepage and, without realizing they're doing it, they run a quick test. Who is this? Is it for me? Can I trust them? What do I do now? They run that test in about five seconds, and if the page doesn't answer it, they leave. Not angry. Not with a complaint. Just gone, off to your competitor, and you never even knew they were there.

After building sites for small businesses, I've stopped thinking of a homepage as a brochure. It's an answer to four questions, asked fast and in this order. Get them right and a plain site will out-earn a gorgeous one that leaves people guessing.

## 1. What is this?

Say what you do in plain words, right at the top, before anyone has to scroll. This is where clever taglines go to die. "Elevating everyday moments" tells me nothing. "Wood-fired pizza in downtown Temecula" tells me everything. If a stranger can't tell what you do in a couple of seconds, they won't stick around to figure it out.

**Do this:** One clear line at the top that names what you do and, ideally, where. Save the personality for after you've earned the read.

## 2. Who is it for?

The right visitor should feel like the page was built for them. That means naming your customer or your area out loud. "Family law for parents going through divorce" or "serving the Inland Empire" does more than a page of polish, because it tells the right person they're in the right place, and quietly tells the wrong ones they aren't. That's a feature, not a bug.

**Do this:** Speak to one specific customer, not "everyone." Everyone is nobody.

## 3. Can I trust you?

People decide whether your business is legit in about half a second, and they decide it before they read a word. An outdated, generic, or empty page quietly says "maybe don't." You fix that with proof: real reviews, real photos of real work, your credentials, and a real human face. Stock photos and vague promises do the opposite of what you want.

**Do this:** Put your strongest trust signal where they'll see it early. Reviews and real photos beat adjectives every time.

## 4. What do I do next?

A visitor should never have to hunt for the next step. If your homepage doesn't make the one action you want blindingly obvious, you've left it up to chance, and chance loses. Call, book, get a quote, whatever it is, pick one and make it impossible to miss.

**Do this:** One primary action, made into a loud, obvious button, repeated as they scroll. Not five competing links. One.

> The mistake I see most is a homepage that's beautiful and silent. It looks great and says nothing. Pretty was never the job. Answering those four questions, fast, is the job.

If you read this and you're honestly not sure your homepage answers all four in the first five seconds, that's worth a look. It's exactly the kind of thing we fix.
